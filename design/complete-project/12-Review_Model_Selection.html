Current Selection Most common model

Reviewers know the identity of the authors, but authors do not know the identity of reviewers. This is the traditional and most common method of peer review.

Visibility:
  • Reviewers can see author names and affiliations
  • Authors cannot see reviewer identities

Neither the reviewer nor the author knows each other's identity. This approach aims to reduce potential bias in the review process.

Visibility:
  • Reviewers cannot see author names and affiliations
  • Authors cannot see reviewer identities
Emerging Trend

Both reviewers and authors know each other's identity. Review reports may be published alongside the article, promoting transparency in the scientific process.

Visibility:
  • Reviewers can see author names and affiliations
  • Authors can see reviewer identities
  • Review reports may be published with the article
Advanced

Reviewers and editors are unaware of the authors' identities, and authors are unaware of the reviewers' identities. This provides the highest level of anonymity in the review process.

Visibility:
  • Reviewers cannot see author names and affiliations
  • Editors cannot see author names and affiliations
  • Authors cannot see reviewer identities

Help & Resources

About Peer Review Models

Selecting the right peer review model is crucial for maintaining the integrity and quality of your journal. Each model offers different levels of transparency and anonymity.

Double-blind review is often preferred in fields where knowledge of the author's identity might influence the reviewer's assessment.
