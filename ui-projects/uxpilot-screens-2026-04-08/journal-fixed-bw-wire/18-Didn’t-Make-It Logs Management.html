Based on the neural recording data provided, I can identify several patterns that suggest attentional modulation in the corvid nidopallium caudolaterale (NCL) that parallel what we observe in the mammalian prefrontal cortex:

  1. The NCL neurons show increased firing rates during the focused attention condition compared to the divided attention condition, similar to the attention-dependent rate modulation seen in primate dorsolateral prefrontal cortex.
  2. There appears to be temporal synchronization between certain neuron clusters during successful task completion, which resembles the phase-locking phenomena documented in mammalian models of attention.
  3. The latency of neural responses in condition A (focused attention) is shorter than in condition B (divided attention), suggesting more efficient processing under focused attention.

For follow-up experiments, I would recommend:

  • Simultaneous recording from NCL and sensory areas to investigate top-down modulation
  • Pharmacological manipulation of cholinergic and dopaminergic systems to examine their role in attentional modulation
  • Optogenetic studies to establish causal relationships between NCL activity and attentional performance
  • Comparative studies with other corvid species to assess evolutionary conservation of these mechanisms
