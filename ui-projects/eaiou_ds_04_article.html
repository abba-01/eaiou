Abstract

We present a systematic analysis of correlated error sources underlying the Hubble tension — the ≈5.8σ discrepancy between H₀ = 67.4 ± 0.5 km s⁻¹ Mpc⁻¹ (Planck CMB) and H₀ = 73.2 ± 1.3 km s⁻¹ Mpc⁻¹ (SH0ES distance ladder).[1,2] Contrary to the prevailing assumption of full independence between these measurement pipelines, we identify a shared systematic factor in Cepheid calibration and CMB lensing reconstruction (β = 0.23 ± 0.04) that partially but not wholly explains the observed discrepancy.

1. Introduction

The Hubble constant H₀ parameterizes the present-day expansion rate of the universe and has been measured with increasing precision over three decades.[3] The emergence of a statistically significant tension between early-universe (CMB-based) and late-universe (distance-ladder) measurements constitutes one of the most compelling anomalies in contemporary cosmology, with implications ranging from new relativistic species to modifications of dark energy dynamics.

Prior analyses have universally treated the CMB and distance-ladder measurements as fully statistically independent — a premise we argue requires reexamination given the shared calibration infrastructure across major survey telescopes and the common angular power spectrum pipeline components employed in both CMB and galaxy lensing analyses.

2. Methods

2.1 Cross-pipeline systematic analysis

We compiled systematic error budgets from seven independent H₀ measurement pipelines (Planck 2018, ACT DR6, DESI BAO, SH0ES 2024, TRGB, MIRAS, and surface brightness fluctuations). For each pair, we constructed a correlation matrix of shared calibration inputs, applying a factor analysis model with k=3 latent dimensions to isolate common systematic factors from independent measurement error.

eaiou Policy · Constitutional Doctrine
Temporal Blindness Doctrine
How eaiou seals submission dates to protect authors from recency bias and preserve the integrity of quality-based discovery.
"The date a paper was submitted is not a measure of its quality. Showing it is an invitation to judge by arrival, not by insight."
01
Submission timestamps are sealed at receipt
When a paper enters eaiou (the CAUGHT event), the exact receipt timestamp is recorded in the immutable hash chain and never exposed through any public interface. The timestamp is available to editors for administration but cannot be queried, exported, or inferred through any public API. This seal is permanent and irrevocable.
02
Discovery sort order is q_signal DESC only
All public paper listings — browse, search, gap maps, trending — sort exclusively by q_signal (provenance quality score, 0–10). No date-based sort is exposed to readers, and no interface element reveals relative recency. Papers from one year ago and ten years ago appear equally, ordered by demonstrable quality of investigation and gap coverage.
03
Gap age amplifies — recency does not
The one temporal factor that does affect scores is gap age: a gap that has been unaddressed for longer amplifies the gap_coverage dimension score via the age amplifier function age_amp = 1.0 + min(age_years/15, 0.8), capping at ×1.80. This rewards researchers who address longstanding open problems, not those who publish fastest.
04
The Status Tracker shows process, not time
The public-facing /papers/status/{id} page shows workflow state (NAUGHT · CAUGHT · TRIAGE · REVIEW · DECISION · FOUND) without any timestamps. Authors receive private confirmation of each transition; the public sees only the current stage.
